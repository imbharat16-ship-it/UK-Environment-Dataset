### John (Phase 3 · Extend)

**Profile:** Married man with two daughters (ages 5 and 7) living in an 1890s Victorian-era house in [home town] for the past six years. Works full time (Mon–Fri, nine to five) in the voluntary sector as a grants officer for a regional charity; in the office three days a week and from home two days (Mondays and Fridays). Wife works fully from home; increasingly shares cooking duties, particularly on evenings when John returns late. Left school at 16, worked in retail, photography, education, and the voluntary sector; brief history of leading beach cleans for a local environmental charity, which first made him consciously aware of plastic waste. Self-describes as a "passive activist" on environmental issues: aware and supportive of change, but relies on industry and media pressure rather than boycotts or protest. Shops at Asda, Tesco, Morrisons depending on route; does multiple small shops per week rather than one big weekly shop; uses deal apps (Shopmium, Green Jinn, Checkout Smart) to stock free or discounted items including vegan milk and hard seltzers. Meal plans every week. Household aspires to reduce meat consumption — Meat-Free Mondays plus Quorn-based meals — but diet is generally convenience-oriented: frozen pizza, jarred Bolognese sauce, microwavable rice, chicken substitute dippers. Father-in-law passed away during Week 2 of the study, significantly disrupting diary Week 2.

#### Interview 1 (Step 0) — 20,989 words · 107,100 characters

**Themes:**
- **Household/home:** Victorian-era detached house in [home town], 6 years. Large American-style fridge-freezer (freezer completely packed). Pantry/larder for dry and tinned goods. Kitchen has upper cabinets added by John's family. Dining room adjacent to kitchen. Wood burner in sitting room, barely used. No dishwasher.
- **Family and work:** Father of two primary-school daughters (5 and 7). Wife works from home all the time; John goes to office 3 days/week. Takes daughters to school on Mondays and Fridays (home days). Commuting to [x] office when in-person = roughly one full day of lost time per week. Finds home working days more productive.
- **Beach cleans history:** Worked for a local environmental charity early in voluntary sector career; led beach cleans; picking up ear buds, packaging, and international ocean debris first made plastic waste viscerally real for him: "once you start seeing it on the beaches, even though I haven't done that for many years, it probably planted a seed."
- **Recycling system:** Waste bin immediately inside kitchen door; recycling (paper, glass, tins, plastic) goes in council recycling boxes outside the back door. Items that are dirty or smell are rinsed first (on the windowsill while still wet from use); milk bottles, beer bottles, yoghurt pots. Rule: if it would go mouldy or smell, rinse it. Does not wash to food-safe standard but until the water runs clear. Fortnightly kerbside collection.
- **Recycling heuristics:** Hard plastic = likely recyclable; soft/flimsy plastic = bin. Looks for the recycling triangle symbol but does not systematically check the number. Has used the [x] City Council website to identify which plastics (1, 5 and 6) are accepted. "Does the rip test" — rips packaging to see if there is a thin clingfilm-like layer indicating a plastic coating on paper. Uses "feel" and shininess as cues.
- **Shopping habits:** Multiple small shops per week (to save time with the girls); ties shopping to route home from work (Tesco Carnforth off the motorway or Morrisons). Keeps a running meal plan; cross-references against fridge/freezer/cupboard before shopping; has a list but always checks end-aisle deals and the "whoopsie" aisle. Buys reduced items he knows the family will eat. "I love finding a bargain."
- **Deal apps:** Uses Shopmium, Green Jinn, Checkout Smart; has accumulated months of free vegan/oat milk via these apps for office use; colleagues now share his supply. Has a BrewDog beer subscription (lapsing due to ethical concerns); buys multipacks of pop via apps but is committing to reducing pop at home.
- **Meal patterns:** Broadly flexitarian — Meat-Free Mondays evolved into multiple meat-free meals per week. Quorn mince, chicken-substitute dippers, halloumi burgers. Weekly pizza (frozen margherita — "I call it a treat but it's the most guilt-inducing meal of the week"). Pasta and jarred sauce. Curries with microwavable rice (stocked up from deals). Girls eat the same meals as adults; family has "acclimated the menu to match theirs." Sunday roast occasionally.
- **Apps and shopping impulse:** Admits to over-stocking from deals. Pantry/cupboard visible in videos includes 6+ months of vegan milk, dozens of microwavable rice pouches including coconut-flavoured ones no one likes much. Former retail experience makes him aware of over-stocking and stock rotation.
- **Girls' breakfasts:** Girls are in the phase of Peppa Pig yoghurts and fruit puree pouches ("they suck the fruit puree out"). John acknowledges "they're horrible the amount of plastic on them" but it is quick and cheap for the morning rush. Apple slices or bananas go to school.
- **Tetrapaks and oat milk:** Tetrapaks from oat milk have been going in the bin after John concluded they are not suitable for the council boxes. Does not take them to specialist recycling points ("two miles down the road, only one point"). Sees the transport burden as a structural barrier, not a personal failing.
- **Environmental philosophy:** Genuinely aware and concerned but believes change must come through industry momentum and consumer pressure rather than individual heroics. "Passive activist" — will switch brand if it is the last holdout (Yorkshire tea and plastic tea bags), but is patient (gives it 2 years not 5). Media has had a real effect — Greta Thunberg, plastic-free places campaigns.
- **Sniff test:** Uses smell (and visible curdling) to judge milk past use-by date; eats out-of-date yoghurt himself but would not give it to the girls; wife is an adult and makes her own decisions.
- **Christmas / large shops:** Christmas generated significantly more recycling (cardboard boxes). Uses a repurposed cardboard box to put paper recycling in when volume is high.

**Behavioral lenses:** Identity ("passive activist," former beach-cleaner, father of daughters), Pricing/value (deal apps, end-aisle bargains, whoopsie aisle, cost-conscious weekly planning), Convenience tradeoff (multiple small shops vs. one big shop, microwavable rice, jarred sauce), Recycling diligence ("rip test," council website check, windowsill rinse routine), Food waste sensitivity (sniff test, stock-rotation awareness, use-by date flexibility), Culture/social (Meat-Free Mondays, girls' food preferences, beer subscription social identity), Attitude-behavior gap (knows about tetrapak specialist recycling, doesn't do it; beer/pop deals undermine reduce-plastic intent).

#### Follow-up interview 2 (Step —) — 3,540 words · 19,442 characters

**Themes:**
- **Pantry video — identifying plastics:** Shows two categories he has mentally separated: "paper-like" outer packaging (cous cous, mug shots) and "shiny" ones (sauces). Describes a foil-like interior on the paper-outer ones. Uses the "rip test" to detect clingfilm-like plastic lining even when exterior looks and tears like paper. Compares to knowing that wrapping paper with metallic print often has a thin plastic film.
- **Mixed-material packaging destiny:** If packaging has multiple materials and he is unsure, it goes in the bin. Knows Terra Cycle handles this but finds it inaccessible ("specialist recycling, few and far between"). Accepts that items go in the bin when they don't clearly meet council criteria.
- **Recycling labelling hierarchy:** Notes that "it seems fairly standard these days" for packaging to list all components with individual disposal instructions (tray/recycle, sleeve/recycle, film/don't recycle). Uses the Quorn burger packaging as an example. Comfortable following this if it is explicit.
- **Microwavable rice — portion logic:** Two pouches for four people (curry night). Girls share less than one between them; John and wife share one-and-a-bit. Tries not to over-portion. Has stocked "way more than ordinarily" due to deal-aisle impulses; coconut-flavoured ones mounting up. Former retail job makes stock-rotation awareness sharp.
- **Plastic in teabags:** Aware that teabags may contain plastic. Brand-loyal to Yorkshire Tea. Packaging is not explicit — he suspects that means they probably still contain plastic. Is "patient" with the change — expects Yorkshire Tea to follow PG Tips' move within 2 years. Calls himself a "passive activist." Would not stand outside their office with a picket sign.
- **Sniff test and use-by dates:** Applies sniff test to milk past use-by. Would eat out-of-date yoghurt himself but not give to the girls. Wife makes her own decisions. Not about risk for him — about protecting children first.
- **Meat packaging not recycled:** Confirms he does not rinse and recycle raw meat packaging (chicken, mince, bacon, ~once/month now). Does rinse meat-substitute packaging. "I'm aware of e-coli and things like that."
- **Plastic shock:** If asked before the study how much of his food shop was in plastic he would have said 50% or less; now estimates 90% or more. "I'm doing things with a slightly different lens." Photographing his shopping on the conveyor belt was genuinely unusual.
- **Apple preferences:** Girls want specific apple varieties that only come prepacked. Loose apples for adults.
- **McDonald's reducing plastics:** Views positively. Happy Meal toys: Kinder egg comparison — girls enjoy them for 5 minutes. "I'm glad to see the back of it." References George Monbiot's argument that paper straws won't change the world but defends the direction: "Across McDonald's, multiplied by thousands in every country... we are seeing a huge reduction in it."
- **Carrier-bag recycling point:** Used it once (lettuce wrapper). Is not sure if he'll keep it up. "I've done it once and I haven't seen any more since." Commitment is cautious and honest.

**Behavioral lenses:** Identity ("passive activist," patient change-advocate, former beach-cleaner, reflective planner), Recycling diligence (rip test, packaging component instructions, meat packaging exception, sniff test), Attitude-behavior gap (tetrapak: knows, doesn't do; carrier-bag point: done once, uncertain), Pricing/value (deal impulses, bargain aisle, app-driven over-stocking), Convenience tradeoff (microwavable rice for speed, curries in 15 minutes), Culture/social (girls' apple preferences, beer identity, McDonalds for bereavement comfort).
