### Katie (Phase 3 · Extend)

No children; discovered she is pregnant during the study (confirmed by Follow-up 1, five to six weeks in), which materially reshapes her food and packaging thinking. Kitchen is tiny — no room for a bin, uses carrier bags hanging from the door. Aspires to low-plastic, whole-food, organic-adjacent eating but is constrained by time, cost, nausea (pregnancy), and partner's preferences.

### Step summaries

#### Interview 1 (Step 0) — 15,867 words · 80,612 characters

**Themes:**
- **Household/home:** Small two-bed semi-detached; kitchen so compact there is no room for a bin — waste goes into carrier bags hung from the door handle (one for recycling, one for general waste). No dishwasher. Fridge-freezer is manageable for two people but partner tends to overfill.
- **Environmental identity:** Katie self-describes as "environmentally aware" and holds a Masters in an environmental subject; she is professionally engaged with air quality, water quality, and chemical pollutants. In practice she acknowledges the gap: "I'd love to live more sustainably... but I do buy plastic and I drive a diesel car."
- **Conflict themes:** Water quality vs. packaging — Katie dislikes the starchy, chlorinated taste of tap water in this house; uses a Brita filter jug but knows the cartridges are difficult to recycle and the jug itself is plastic. Occasionally buys 2L bottled mineral water when the filtered water still tastes bad, which she finds ethically uncomfortable. Gut health vs. packaging — buys Actimel (tiny plastic bottles) for herself and partner (he has IBS). Wishes they came in small cardboard cartons.
- **Shopping:** Shops mainly at Asda; partner loves Iceland (freezer fills up). Discovers Shopmium / Green Jinn / Checkout Smart deal apps; gets items free or heavily discounted including regular vegan/coconut milk and hard seltzers. Apps have shifted her from 1–2 large weekly shops to 4–5 smaller trips. Often plans meals around the meat aisle.
- **Food habits:** Katie does most of the cooking; partner cooks "meat and two veg" style, Katie cooks mixed dishes (Spanish chicken, stir fries, etc.) to sneak vegetables in. Both have committed to Meat-Free Mondays; partner eats a lot of chips, crisps, and convenience freezer food. Katie tries to eat 30 different plants per week, inspired by New Scientist microbiome articles. Buys mixed salad bags for variety; buying individual leaves would lead to waste.
- **Recycling system:** Two carrier bags in the kitchen — one for mixed recycling (paper, metal, plastic), one for general waste. Takes recycling out every couple of days when the bag is full. Sorts recycling outside. Does not always wash containers; rough scrape-and-go unless it would be smelly or attract wildlife. Tactile heuristic: hard plastic = likely recyclable; flimsy / film plastic = bin.
- **Plastic vs. environment concerns:** Aware of microplastic accumulation from Masters study. Reads about microplastics and environmental toxins in New Scientist. Feels conflicted: "everything's poisonous." Prefers not to microwave food in plastic (e.g. boil-in-bag) due to chemical leaching concerns, but does not consistently avoid it.
- **Composting:** Composts vegetable scraps and fruit; garden is small and the compost is filling up. Compostable magazine packaging (New Scientist) also composted. Does not compost meat or processed food.
- **Recycling discovery (Brita):** During Interview 1, actually looks at the Brita cartridge while speaking and discovers the instruction to recycle it via Brita. "I've just learnt that now." Did not know about Brita's cartridge return scheme prior to this moment.
- **Cat:** Previously used plastic pouches (lots of waste); cat decided she wanted gourmet "double delicacies" tins instead. Tins now = effectively no plastic cat waste. Saves ring pulls from bottles as cat toys (discovered via The Plastic Bottle Top Project Facebook group).
- **Work plastic:** Environmental work involves ~70 nitrogen-dioxide diffusion tubes collected per month, not reused — "that's about 70 test tubes a month just from our region." Hospital blood-sample lab work (former job) involved hundreds of plastic bags per day incinerated, including unnecessary double-bagging during COVID — found this particularly troubling.

**Behavioral lenses:** Identity (environmental professional, self-described "aware"), Pricing/value (deal apps, reluctant Iceland freezer, yellow-sticker bargains), Convenience tradeoff (Asda vs. farmers' market, instant coffee vs. reusable methods), Attitude-behavior gap (aspires to organic/low-plastic, buys standard Asda), Recycling diligence (tactile heuristic, Brita cartridge discovery in-session), Food waste sensitivity (mixed salad bags to reduce waste, dislikes freezer food going stale).
