#### Interview 1 (Step 0) — 15,509 words · 78,365 characters

**Themes:** Plastic reduction as inherited identity; vegan diet as parallel shift; zero-waste refill culture; household systems; communal recycling friction; compostable packaging scepticism.

Hannah traces her plastic-reduction journey directly to her mother, who ten years ago spent a year collecting every piece of single-use plastic she generated and showed it to the family. Hannah was at university then and couldn't fully commit (cost, no storage space); when postgraduate wages arrived she graduated to zero-waste shopping, toothpaste tablets, natural deodorant, homemade bread, and reusable everything. Veganism followed, motivated partly by ethics, partly by the observation that buying meat and dairy always came with packaging.

The zero-waste shop is the centrepiece of her shopping routine: she plans the monthly trip around it, combining it with a café visit or restaurant. She brings weighed empty containers, bread bags (lighter and easier to fill than jars), bagel bags, and old peanut-butter jars. The shop assistant recognises the family resemblance to her sister, who shops there too. She is evangelical but not preachy about it — she frames it as normative culture-building.

Diet is mostly pasta, rice, noodles, lentils, and legumes; she batch cooks four to eight portions, freezes them, and eats from the freezer. Porridge at work (weighed oats, added water). Sundried tomatoes and olives bought in glass jars (Lidl). Linda McCartney sausages and frozen burgers in cardboard.

Soft plastics: highly sceptical. She knows the tracker story — soft plastic collected in supermarket bags was traced to Poland, not recycled. She still uses the bag for residual unavoidable packaging but treats it as a last resort rather than a solution. Compostable packaging (received as birthday gift — vegan cheese; and used for outdoor energy bars) is accumulated at home or at her parents' compost heap; she has not found a reliable disposal route in her flat.

#### Diary 1 (Step 1) — 2,369 words · 12,953 characters

**Context:** Holiday in Germany (Cologne, Munich, Nuremberg) and Austria (Vienna) at the end of August.

**Summary:** Hannah travels with a full kit — two Keepcups, two Tupperwares (one squashable), mesh veg bags, cotton bag, reusable cutlery, fold-up plastic sandwich wrap, and a peanut butter jar. The diary documents her encounter with German and Austrian packaging norms from the perspective of someone who finds the UK system inadequate.

In Germany: the Mehrweg (deposit return) system is normalised — glass bottles and cans carry a deposit, reverse vending machines at Lidl and Aldi return cash. Bakeries give paper bags as default. Supermarket produce is sold loose. She notes packaging labels on German products directing consumers to specific bins (e.g., the hummus pot said "yellow bin"). In Munich's yellow bin (which takes all plastics) she correctly disposes of a hummus pot she had carried from the UK. She collects her few unavoidable pieces and sorts them at the bin at the supermarket checkout.

In Vienna: a pfand system operates for disposable cups at public events (a deposit keeps people returning cups). No sachets or individual wrappings at a friend's flat. Her Viennese friend's recycling setup has three bins (glass / plastic-and-tins / paper-and-cardboard) plus a food waste caddy — she photographs the system with admiration. Nespresso pods: coffee grounds to food waste, rinsed aluminium capsule to recycling. Water is refilled from Vienna's famous public fountains throughout.

**Themes:** Deposit-return as cultural norm; German recycling labelling on pack; European systems vs UK; travel kit in practice; public water infrastructure.

**Behavioral lens:** Culture/social (German norms vs UK); Recycling diligence (actively seeking bins, using deposit schemes); Reuse tendency (travel kit, fountain refills); Occasion (holiday decisions); Pricing/value (€3.50 bottles vs free fountains).

#### Follow-up interview 2 (Step 7) — 3,291 words · 17,987 characters

**Themes:** Habit reinforcement through penalty (going without); on-the-go stress as identity signal; compostable packaging research and scepticism; zero-waste shop community; bread bags as preferred lightweight container.

Going without as habit: not using a reusable cup means you go without — this penalty mechanism is how the habit became automatic. She frames it as a system rather than willpower. The stress she feels when she cannot avoid packaging she now interprets as a reliable identity signal, not just anxiety.

Boyfriend's parents: she is aware they watch her habits. Progress has been very slow; she has stopped trying to influence them directly. The coffee van business means boyfriend's dad's packaging footprint is structurally large — she separates that from personal choice.

Compostable packaging: she tried the Recycle Now website (found it unsatisfying); believes plant-based materials are better than oil-based plastics in principle; doesn't know how to dispose of them without a home compost or food waste caddy. Accumulates at a corner of her desk or takes to parents' compost heap. Wary of greenwashing — recognises that "compostable" claims are often industrial-compost-only.

Zero-waste shop community: chatted to the shopkeeper; there is informal sharing of extra containers, but no organised system. She notes that some suppliers allow the shop to take back bulk containers; the nutritional yeast she buys comes from a large white bag at the shop (still plastic, but dramatically less per serving).

Bread bags: lighter than glass jars for refill, easy to fill at the counter, two-person system makes it easier (one holds, one fills).

#### Interview 2 — held out (21/09/22) — 11,391 words · 59,078 characters

**Themes:** Definition of single-use plastic; soft plastic scepticism; environmental impact framing; kerbside variability; containers at refill shops and restaurants; retailer responsibility; food waste vs packaging trade-off; partner's parents as slow-change case study.

Hannah defines single-use plastic as packaging used without thinking about end-of-life; she sees cans and tins as different in feel (more recyclable in practice) and crisp packets, bread bags, and film as the clearest examples. Soft plastics she treats as single-use even when a supermarket collection system exists, because she doesn't trust the supply chain — the tracker story (to Poland) defines her view and she does not expect it to be resolved.

Environmental impact framing: litter she notices in hedgerows and parks; Blue Planet as the cultural reference; production is faster than removal. Seasonal eating has been eroded — strawberries imported in winter from flights and heated greenhouses represent a wider unsustainability she connects to packaging excess.

Kerbside: her mum has visited the materials recycling facility (person sorting a conveyor belt); communal flat bins aggregate everything, which reduces her ability to recycle carefully. Glass requires a separate trip to the estate bins — she finds this already annoying; more glass would break the system.

Taking own containers: fine in refill shops where it is expected and welcomed. At a Silverstone coffee shop she tried to hand over a reusable cup and the barista made the coffee in a single-use cup first and then poured it — she felt a failure, not the barista. German bakery paper bag moment: awkward because she asked and the server didn't understand the request.

Retailer responsibility: she believes consumers cannot change alone — retailers need to act. Names the 7g yeast packet as an obvious, pointless piece of single-use packaging. Supermarkets claim green credentials but supply chains don't follow. This is her most politically explicit position in the data.

On-the-go stress: batch cooking and bringing the bread maker wherever she goes are her structural responses. The arena cups are "a lost battle." She has not found a solution for energy gels.
