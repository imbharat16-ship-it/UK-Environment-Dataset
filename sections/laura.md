#### Interview 1 (Step 0) — 14,095 words · 72,493 characters

**Themes:** Household as system; multi-supermarket strategy; flyaway-plastic infrastructure; reuse habits; NHS-shaped identity; anti-food waste ethic.

Laura opens with a detailed tour of her home: listed barn, Aga, large pantry with Kilner jars, utility room with recycling station, garage with flyaway-plastic bag. Her shopping week is a choreographed circuit — parkrun Saturday triggers the Lidl run; near-school pick-ups prompt Sainsburys stops; Ocado runs for the week's bulk. She calls herself a "shopping tart," unashamed of loyalty card juggling and reduced-aisle opportunism. She buys fresh fish and meat on yellow sticker and freezes it immediately.

The anti-food waste ethic is structural: two chickens receive all kitchen scraps; foraging fills summer weekends; three freezers are managed by drawer and shelf system. Her annual vegan January experiment ran once but was not retained — she frames it as evidence of trying rather than failure.

Recycling is split into three streams: kerbside (South Oxfordshire — one of the best in England, accepts all plastics), flyaway bag in garage (soft plastics taken to Tesco cage or supermarket soft-plastic point), and Terracycle for cat food pouches and tetra packs at Sainsburys. She knows all three systems, uses them consistently, and acts as household enforcer — correcting husband (who puts cheese in the flyaway bag without thinking) and occasionally reminding teenage daughter.

Silicone food covers are used daily instead of clingfilm; Kilner jars hold everything from pantry staples to fermented goods; old plastic breast-milk pots (20 years old) still live in the freezer for portions.

#### Follow-up interview 2 (Step 7) — 1,964 words · 10,649 characters

**Themes:** Online shopping and packaging opacity; packaging switching (cardboard ice cream); condensation and produce; Asian bulk shopping; food covers vs clingfilm in practice.

Ice cream switched from plastic tubs to cardboard: she noticed and was pleased, but it did not change her purchase decision. She used to reuse plastic ice cream tubs as lunchboxes; now they are gone. Pak choi in nasty packaging creates condensation — she removes all veg from plastic on arrival home; plastic serves transport/display, not the food's benefit. Rice vinegar and soy sauce: pull ring and plastic wrap are unnecessary; she buys Asian goods in bulk from an Asian supermarket to reduce per-item packaging.

Shopping online removes packaging visibility — she cannot see the wrap before buying. At price equality between two options she will choose less plastic, but price dominates.

Food covers vs clingfilm: uses food covers every day; still reaches for clingfilm only for very oily or chilli-heavy foods, or when transporting (current: blackberry liqueur jars and whipped cream).
